Grokking

How a network can memorize for an eternity, then suddenly understand.

The hook

It learned nothing for ages, then knew everything at once.

Train accuracy hit 100% almost immediately. On unseen problems the model stayed at random guessing, for ten thousand times longer, and then leapt to perfect.

The idea

Memorizing and understanding are two different solutions.

A network can fit its training set with a giant lookup table. That is memorizing. A smaller, cleaner set of weights captures the actual , that is . Grokking is the slow slide from the first to the second.

Think of it like

Cramming versus actually getting it.

Memorize every past exam and you ace those exact papers, yet fail a fresh one. Keep drilling long after you "passed" and one day the underlying method clicks, now any new problem is easy. Grokking is that click, for a neural net.

So what flips it?

A gentle pressure away from memorizing.

The lookup-table solution needs large, messy weights. A small penalty on weight size, , keeps nudging the network until the cheaper rule wins. Too little and it never flips; too much and it learns nothing.

The payoff

Generalization can lag memorization by orders of magnitude.

The loss curve looked finished long before the model actually understood. "Done training" and "actually learned the rule" are not the same moment.

Why it matters

It cracks open the black box of learning.

Grokking gave researchers a clean, reproducible case where you can watch a network move from memorizing to a real algorithm, a rare window into how understanding forms.

Recap

Three things to carry out.

1 · Networks can memorize first and generalize much, much later.
2 · Weight decay is the quiet force that tips them toward the rule.
3 · A flat loss curve doesn't mean learning has stopped.
